Gallery

Images of various activists and inspirational women who have had and continue to make a difference.

Emmeline Pankhurst

Emmeline Pankhurst founded the Women’s Social and Political Union in 1918. The members, known as the Suffragettes, fought to enfranchise women in the United Kingdom. Pankhurst was imprisoned many times due to her efforts. Parliament granted British women limited suffrage in 1918. Pankhurst died in 1928, shorty before women were given full voting rights.

Angela Davis (1944-), Academic Activist, Civil Rights Activist, Scholar, Women’s Rights Activist

Angela Davis emerged as a radical activist in the 1960s as a leader in the Communist Party USA, and was affiliated with the Black Panther Party through her involvement in the Civil Rights Movement. She was a professor (now retired) at the University of California, Santa Cruz, as a director of the university’s Feminist Studies department and the History of Consciousness Department. Davis not only understands the vital nature of intersectional feminism-she embodies it throughout all her activism. Davis is heavily involved in activism pertaining to prisoner’s rights and solidarity with Palestine and was an important figure in the Occupy Wall Street movement.

Gloria Steinman (1934-), Activist, Women’s Rights Activist, Journalist

Gloria Steinem has been an outspoken champion of women’s rights since the late 1960s. She helped create both New York and Ms. Magazines, which focused on women’s rights and tackled issues like domestic violence. She is the author of many books and essays that helped push the liberation movement forward. Through her work, she championed female independence and social justice, and encouraged women to find their voices.

Rosa Parks (1913-2005), Civil Rights Activist

Rose Parks was an activist in the Civil Rights Movement and whom the United States Congress called “the first lady of civil rights” and “the mother of the freedom movement”. On December 1, 1955, in Montgomery Alabama, Parks refused to obey the bus driver’s orders to give up her seat in the colored section to a white passenger, after the white section was filled. Parks became an important symbol of desegregation and protest.

Malala Yousafzai (1997-present), Children’s Activist, Women’s Rights Activist

Malala defied the Taliban in Pakistan and demanded that girls be allowed to receive an education. She wrote a blog for the BBC detailing the conditions of the education system in her country and why it was essential for young women to go to school. Due to her activism, she was shot in the head by a Taliban gunman in 2012 but survived. Since her recovery, she has become one of the most recognized education advocates in the world. She has also received the Nobel Peace Prize in 2014, becoming the youngest person to receive the award.

Wangari Maathai, (1947-2011), Environmental Activist, Women’s Right’s Activist

Wangari Maathai was a Kenyan-born activist who founded the Green Belt Movement, an environmental organization focused on the planting on trees, conservation and women’s rights. Maathai became the first African women to win the Nobel Peace Prize in 2004 for her contribution to sustainability and Peace. She dedicated her life encouraging countries in Africa to plant trees and built plant nurseries as they would combat issues such as deforestation, hunger and water crises.

Audre Lorde (1934-1992), Writer, Poet, Feminist, Womanist, Civil Right’s Activist

Audre Lorde once described herself as a “black lesbian feminist mother warrior poet” and through her work; she has been an inspiration to many. As a poet, she is best known for technical mastery and emotional expression, as well as her poems that express anger and outrage at civil and social injustices she observed throughout her life. Her poems and prose largely deal with issues related to civil rights, feminism, and the exploration of black female identity.

Laverne Cox, American actress, LGBT+ advocate

Proximately known for her role on Netflix series Orange Is The New Black as Sophia Burset, Laverne Cox is the first transgender woman of colour to have a leading role on a mainstream scripted TV show. Laverne Cox has been noted by her LGBT+ peers, and many others, for being a trailblazer in the transgender community and has won numerous awards for her activism as she frequently speaks out about transgender rights and also the intersections of transphobia, racism and misogyny.
